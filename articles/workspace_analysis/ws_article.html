Workspace Analysis

What is Workspace Analysis?

Most robotic manipulators are designed to move the end effector in 3D space to perform tasks like pick and place,assembly and welding.Before fixing the robot for any application,it is important to analyze where the robot end effector can reach and how effectively it can move within the interested regions.

Workspace analysis is a fundamental step in robot design and task allocation.It allows to determine the operational limits of the robot and evaluate whether it can perform the desired task before deployment.

Why is Workspace Analysis Important

A robot may be able to reach a task point,but successful execution of opertaion depends on more than reachability.The manipulator must also possess dexterity to orient the end-effector,avoid singualr configurations, and respect joint limits.

Workspace analysis answers :

  • Can robot reach every point required by the task?
  • Which regions provide good dexterity?
  • How do joint limits influence the workspace?
  • Is the selected manipulator suitable for desired task?
These are valuable during robot selection,workspace design and task design.

Reachable Workspace

The most basic workspace analysis is reachable workspace

The reachable workspace consists of all psoitions that the robot's end-effector can attain through at least one configuration.It does not focus on how orientations are possible at each configuration.

In practice, reachable workspaces are commonly generated using Monte Carlo sampling,where thousands of random joint configurations are sampled within the joint limits.Forward Kinematics is evaluated for each configuration,and the resulting end effector positions collectively approximate the robots reachable volume.

Beyound Reachability

Although reachability is important, it is rarely sufficient for real-world robotic applications.

Two positions may both lie inside the reachable workspace, yet one position may allow the robot to move freely in many directions while the other lies close to a singular configuration where motion becomes difficult or unstable.

For applications requiring precise manipulation, force control, or continuous motion, we are interested not only in whether a point can be reached, but also in how effectively the robot can move once it reaches that point.

This motivates the study of manipulator dexterity through manipulability analysis.

Manipulability Workspace

A manipulability workspace extends the concept of a reachable workspace by associating each reachable position with a measure of the robot's kinematic dexterity.

Instead of simply observing whether a point is reachable, a manipulability metric such as Yoshikawa's manipulability index is evaluated from the manipulator Jacobian at each sampled configuration. Regions with higher manipulability correspond to configurations where the robot can generate motion more freely in different directions, while regions with lower manipulability indicate reduced dexterity and proximity to singularities.

Why Not Plot the Dextrous Workspace?

The dextrous workspace is theoretically defined as the set of positions where the end-effector can achieve every possible orientation.

For redundant manipulators, verifying this condition is computationally extensive because each position corresponds to infinitely many joint configurations and continuously varying orientationsChecking every orientation at every reachable point is therefore impractical.

Consequently, we do not explicitly compute the dextrous workspace for high-DOF manipulators. Instead, manipulability analysis provides a computationally efficient approximation of dexterity, offering meaningful insight into the quality of motion throughout the workspace without requiring exhaustive orientation verification.
